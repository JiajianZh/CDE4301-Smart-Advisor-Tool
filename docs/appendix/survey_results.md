## Key Findings

### Primary Research Question

**Q1: Are you certain about which university programme you want to apply to?**

| Response | Count | Percentage |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Very certain - I know exactly what I want | 8 | 18.2% |
| Somewhat certain - I have a few options in mind | 15 | 34.1% |
| Uncertain - I'm exploring different options | 14 | 31.8% |
| Very uncertain - I have no idea what to choose | 7 | 15.9% |

**Key insight:** 21 out of 44 respondents (47.7%) reported being uncertain or very uncertain about their programme choice. This closely aligns with the global finding that approximately 40% of students experience career uncertainty.

---

### Supporting Questions

**Q2: What makes choosing a university programme difficult for you?**

*(Multiple choice, respondents could select multiple options)*

| Difficulty | Count | Percentage |
|------------|-------|------------|
| Too many options to choose from | 32 | 72.7% |
| Don't know what I'm interested in | 18 | 40.9% |
| Worried about job prospects | 26 | 59.1% |
| Don't understand what different programmes involve | 22 | 50.0% |
| Pressure from parents/teachers | 15 | 34.1% |
| Worried about academic difficulty | 19 | 43.2% |
| Don't know if I'll enjoy the programme | 24 | 54.5% |

**Key insight:** The most common challenge is information overload (72.7%), followed by concerns about job prospects (59.1%) and uncertainty about enjoyment/fit (54.5%).

## Limitations

### Sampling Limitations

1. **Small sample size:** 44 respondents is not representative of the entire pre-university student population in Singapore.

2. **Snowball sampling bias:** Respondents were recruited through personal networks, which may overrepresent certain demographic groups (e.g., students from specific schools or socioeconomic backgrounds).

3. **Self-selection bias:** Students who voluntarily completed the survey may be more engaged with the topic of programme choice than the general population.

4. **Geographic limitation:** No data on which schools respondents attend or their geographic distribution within Singapore.

5. **No longitudinal tracking:** Survey captured a single point in time, not how student certainty changes over time.

### Survey Design Limitations

1. **No validation of "uncertainty":** Students self-reported uncertainty, but no objective measure was used to validate this.

2. **Limited demographic data:** Did not collect data on academic performance, socioeconomic status, or parental education level, which may influence programme choice confidence.

3. **No comparison group:** No data from students who have already chosen and enrolled in university programmes to compare satisfaction levels.

## Comparison to Existing Research

### Alignment with Global Findings

- **This survey:** 47.7% uncertain about programme choice
- **OECD (2025):** ~40% of students uncertain about career direction
- **NIE Singapore (2023):** 25% regret their education pathway choice

**Conclusion:** Despite the small, non-representative sample, the uncertainty rate observed in this survey is consistent with larger, peer-reviewed studies. This suggests that student uncertainty about programme choice is a real and significant problem, not an artifact of sampling.

---

## Implications for Tool Design

### Survey Insights Applied to Development

1. **Information overload → Comprehensive coverage:** Tool covers all 75 NUS programmes to reduce information gathering burden.

2. **Don't know what programmes involve → Detailed descriptions:** Each programme receives value tags and drill-down questions to reveal day-to-day activities.

3. **Worried about enjoyment/fit → Interest-based matching:** RIASEC framework specifically targets person-programme fit to increase confidence in match quality.

4. **Want interest-based matching → Validated framework:** Using Holland's RIASEC theory (70+ years of research) instead of arbitrary personality quizzes.

5. **Comparison between similar programmes → Drill-down system:** Two-stage approach addresses the "engineering programmes all sound similar" problem.

---

## Future Survey Work

### Recommended Follow-Up Studies

1. **Larger sample validation:** Recruit 200+ respondents through formal channels (schools, NUS Admissions).

2. **Post-tool usage survey:** Measure decision confidence before and after using the Smart Advisor Tool.

3. **Longitudinal outcome tracking:** Follow students who used the tool into university and measure satisfaction, persistence, and performance.

4. **Comparison study:** Compare tool users vs. students who made decisions through traditional advising only.
